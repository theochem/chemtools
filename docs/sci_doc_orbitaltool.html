Electron Localisation Function¶

The concepts of chemical bonds and electron pairs are fundamental in our understanding of chemistry. Quantum chemical calculations, however, yield canonical orbitals which are delocalized in space and do not contain this chemical information at first sight. There are different schemes to generate localized orbitals by means of an unitary transformation of the canonical orbitals, but none of these schemes are unique.

The Electron Localization Function (ELF), introduced by A. D. Becke and K. E. Edgecombe, was introduced as an alternative to these unitary transformations. It measures the likelihood of finding an electron with the same spin in the neighbourhood of a reference electron. The derivation of ELF starts from the conditional probability \(P^{\sigma \sigma}_{cond} (\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r}')\) to find an electron with \(\sigma\) spin at position \(\mathbf{r}'\) when a reference electron with the same spin is found at \(\mathbf{r}\). A. D. Becke showed that, when \(\mathbf{r}'\) approaches \(\mathbf{r}\), the leading term in the Taylor expansion of the spherically averaged pair probability is:

\[P^{\sigma \sigma}_{cond} (\mathbf{r},s) = \frac{1}{3} \lbrack \tau_{\sigma} (\mathbf{r}) - \frac{1}{4} \frac{(\nabla \rho_{\sigma})^2}{\rho_{\sigma}} \rbrack s^2\]

where \((\mathbf{r},s)\) denotes the average on a sphere of radius \(s\) around the reference electron at \((\mathbf{r}\) and \(\tau_{\sigma}\) is the positive definite kinetic energy density:

\[\tau_{\sigma} (\mathbf{r}) = \sum_i^{\sigma} \lvert \nabla \phi_i (\mathbf{r}) \rvert^2\]

The smaller the probability of finding a second electron with the same spin near the reference electron, the more localized the reference electron, so the information on the electron localisation can thus be found by:

\[D_{\sigma} (\mathbf{r}) = \tau_{\sigma} (\mathbf{r}) - \frac{1}{4} \frac{(\nabla \rho_{\sigma})^2}{\rho_{\sigma}} .\]

There is an inverse relation between the electron localisation and \(D_{\sigma}\): highly localized electrons can be found in regions of small \(D_{\sigma}\). Moreover, \(D_{\sigma}\) is not bounded from above. For these reasons A. D. Becke and K. E. Edgecombe proposed the following formula for the Electron Localization Function:

\[ELF (\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{\left( 1 + \left(\frac{D_{\sigma}(\mathbf{r})} {D_{\sigma}^0 (\mathbf{r})} \right)^2\right)},\]

where \(D_{\sigma}^0\) corresponds to the uniform electron gas:

\[D_{\sigma}^0 (\mathbf{r}) = \frac{3}{5} (6 \pi^2)^{2/3} \rho_{\sigma}^{5/3} (\mathbf{r}) ,\]

making the ratio of \(D_{\sigma} / D_{\sigma}^0\) dimensionless and scaled with respect to the uniform electron gas. As such, the ELF is always in the range

\[0 \leq ELF \leq 1 ,\]

with ELF = 1 corresponding to the perfectly localised reference electron and ELF = \(^1/_2\) to the uniform electron gas-like correlation.

References:

  • A.D. Becke and K.E. Edgecombe. A simple measure of electron localization in atomic and molecular systems. J. Chem. Phys., 92(9):5397–5403, 1990. doi:10.1063/1.458517.
  • A. Savin, A. D. Becke, J. Flad, R. Nesper, H. Preuss, and H. G. von Schnering. A new look at electron localization. Angew. Chem. Int. Ed. Engl., 30(4):409–412, 1991. doi:10.1002/anie.199104091.
